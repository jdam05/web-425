Who was Erich Fromm?

Erich Fromm was a German-born social psychologist, psychoanalyst, sociologist, philosopher, and democratic socialist. Born on March 23, 1900, in Frankfurt, Germany, he was a leading figure in the field of psychology and social theory. Fromm was a German Jew who was forced to flee the Nazi regime, eventually settling in the United States where he continued his work. In New York City, Fromm became one of the founders of the William Alanson White Institute of Psychiatry, Psychoanalysis and Psychology. Fromm was a humanistic philosopher who believed in the importance of individual freedom and autonomy. He rejected the traditional notion of psychoanalysis that saw human beings as passive and determined by unconscious forces, and instead, emphasized the role of individuals in shaping their own destiny. Erich Fromm passed away on March 18, 1980, but his legacy continues to shape the field of psychology and the larger conversations about human freedom, autonomy, and social justice.
